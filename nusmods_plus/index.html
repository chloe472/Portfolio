NUSMods+

NUSMods+ offers a real-time collaborative workspace that allows students to visualise, propose, and confirm tutorial slots together. The concept extends individual timetable planning into a shared coordination flow for friends navigating course registration.

01 Discover

We interviewed four Year 1 to Year 4 NUS undergraduates who coordinate classes with friends during course registration planning. The research focused on small friend groups of two or more students who share overlapping modules and actively try to secure common tutorial slots before and during bidding.

Without a shared visual space, students manually compare screenshots, list viable slot combinations, and negotiate through repeated back-and-forth messages. This effort becomes much heavier as group size increases.

Personas

Persona Context Pain Points Wants
1. Chloe
(Group Coordinator)
Year 1 NUS undergraduate who wants to secure the exact same tutorial slot with a specific group of friends. Messy group chats bury scheduling decisions, and manually comparing multiple screenshots is mentally draining. A single platform to visually plan and coordinate multiple timetables, quickly revealing overlapping available slots.
2. Kai
(1-on-1 Negotiator)
Year 3 NUS undergraduate who coordinates different modules with different friends across several chat threads. Context switching across modules and chats creates overload, and agreements with one friend can clash with another. A focused UI that filters out noise, prevents clashes, and makes sure the chosen tutorial matches his friend's exactly.

02 Define

NUS students lack a shared, real-time workspace for collaborative timetable exploration during the course registration planning phase.

NUSMods

Strong for individual timetable planning, but not designed for live group negotiation.

Google Calendar

Useful after bidding is done, but too cumbersome for testing multiple tutorial combinations.

Telegram

Handles communication, but decisions get buried and visual comparison still happens manually.

03 Develop

NUSMods+ acts as a collaborative workspace where students can share, compare, and jointly edit timetables. Users retain personalised views while negotiating changes through a structured Propose, Preview, Opt-In/Opt-Out flow that updates schedules only after explicit agreement.

Conceptual Model

1

Transparency Sheet Overlays

Each student's timetable acts like a transparent sheet layered over the user's base timetable, making overlaps and clashes immediately visible.

2

System-Vetted Proposals

Students propose alternative tutorial slots from options that the system has already checked for clashes and alignment.

3

Explicit Consensus

Changes are strictly opt-in. Friends can preview proposals in their own schedules before accepting or declining.

04 Validate

We conducted usability testing with four NUS undergraduates who regularly coordinate timetables with friends: three Year 2 students and one Year 4 student. Participants completed four core tasks while following a think-aloud protocol.

  • Compare your schedule with a friend and view both timetables together.
  • Join a friend's session and view both timetables together.
  • Propose having the same tutorial slot, T02, as your friend.
  • Accept or decline a slot proposal from a friend.

Heuristic Evaluation

Issue Heuristic Concern Proposed Solution
Proposal lists become hard to scan when many proposals exist within a module. Recognition rather than recall; flexibility and efficiency of use. Add filtering and sorting by status, module, proposer, or date submitted.
Students cannot explain why they are proposing a new timeslot. Flexibility and efficiency of use. Add an optional reason field so collaborators can understand the context before deciding.
The "Resolve Unalignment" button only appears when a conflict is detected. Visibility of system status; flexibility and efficiency of use. Replace it with a persistent "+ New Proposal" button, using visual treatment to distinguish aligned and unaligned modules.
Invites and proposals are only surfaced by email. Visibility of system status; consistency and standards. Add an in-platform notification bell while retaining email as an external reminder.

Next Steps & Lessons

Next Iteration

Design a less confusing entry point for comparing timetables with friends.

Improvements

Implement proposed heuristic fixes, including proposal filters, optional reasons, and in-platform alerts.

Lessons Learned

Test assumptions early, listen for user problems over user-prescribed solutions, and validate before building too far.
